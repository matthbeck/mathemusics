    Section 3 Scales

    The mathematics of musical scales and, by extension, intervals between two notes has been extensively studied (see, e.g., [5, 14] for two recent papers). Here we present various constructions; all are for a scale in 12 notes in an octave (with frequency ratio \(\frac 2 1\)) and the existence of a (perfect) fifth with frequency ratio \(\frac 3 2\text{.}\) In our simple mathematical model, the fifth corresponds to the number \(7\) in \(\ZZ_{ 12 }\text{.}\)

    Subsection 3.1 The Pythagorean scale

    Our first construction takes the fact that \(7\text{,}\) corresponding to the fifth, generates \(\ZZ_{ 12 }\) quite literally. Again, we assign \(7\) the frequency \(\frac 3 2\text{.}\) Since \(7 + 7 = 2 \in \ZZ_{ 12 }\text{,}\) the frequency assigned to \(2\) (the whole tone) needs to be \((\frac 3 2)^2\) scaled down by an octave, that is, \(\frac 9 8\text{.}\) Moving up by another perfect fifth, we reach \(2 + 7 = 9 \in \ZZ_{ 12 }\) (the major sixth), with frequency \(\frac 9 8 \cdot \frac 3 2 = \frac{ 27 }{ 16 }\text{.}\) The next step \(9 + 7 = 4 \in \ZZ_{ 12 }\) again needs its frequency scaled down by an octave, i.e., \(\frac{ 27 }{ 16 } \cdot \frac 3 2 \cdot \frac 1 2 = \frac{ 81 }{ 64 }\text{,}\) the frequency for the major third. Stepping up one more fifth reaches \(4 + 7 = 11 \in \ZZ_{ 12 }\) (the major seventh), with frequency \(\frac{ 81 }{ 64 } \cdot \frac 3 2 = \frac{ 243 }{ 128 }\text{.}\) One more step yields \(11 + 7 = 6 \in \ZZ_{ 12 }\) (the tritone), with frequency \(\frac{ 243 }{ 128 } \cdot \frac 3 2 \cdot \frac 1 2 = \frac{ 729 }{ 512 }\text{.}\)
    Next we make use of the fact that \(5 = -7 \in \ZZ_{ 12 }\) (the forth), which thus gets assigned the frequency \(\frac 4 3\) (because \(\frac 3 2 \cdot \frac 4 3 = 2\)). From here we now move down a fifth to \(5 - 7 = 10 \in \ZZ_{ 12 }\) (the minor seventh), whose frequency needs to be moved up an octave: \(\frac 4 3 \cdot \frac 2 3 \cdot 2 = \frac{ 16 }{ 9 }\text{.}\) The next fifth down reaches \(10 - 7 = 3 \in \ZZ_{ 12 }\) (the minor third), with frequency \(\frac{ 16 }{ 9 } \cdot \frac 2 3 = \frac{ 32 }{ 27 }\text{.}\) One more step yields \(3 - 7 = 8 \in \ZZ_{ 12 }\) (the minor sixth), with frequency \(\frac{ 32 }{ 27 } \cdot \frac 2 3 \cdot 2 = \frac{ 128 }{ 81 }\text{.}\) Finally, \(8 - 7 = 1 \in \ZZ_{ 12 }\) gives the semitone with frequency \(\frac{ 128 }{ 81 } \cdot \frac 2 3 = \frac{ 256 }{ 243 }\text{.}\) The resulting scale is summarized in Table 3.1.
    At this point, we can see the number theory problem of the circle of fifths from Section 2.4 in action: the next step going down a fifth reaches \(1 - 7 = 6 \in \ZZ_{ 12 }\text{,}\) the tritone, which we already reached by moving up by six fifths.     Subsection 3.2 Just scales

    It is not hard to notice that the frequency ratios in Table 3.1 contain only powers of \(2\) and \(3 \text{;}\) this stems from the very construction of the Pythagorean scale, which in turn necessitates that the numerators and denominators appearing in the frequency ratios become large rather quickly. Our next scale construction avoids this, essentially by also allowing a \(5 \) in the prime factorizations of the frequency values. This feature of building up frequency ratios using the primes \(2 \text{,}\) \(3 \text{,}\) and \(5 \) yields the most restrictive definition of a just scale; a more loose definition of this term applies to any scale whose frequency ratios feature small numerators and denominators.
    In Section 2.3, we motivated the frequency ratio \(\frac 3 2\) of the fifth (corresponding to the number \(7\) in \(\ZZ_{ 12 }\)), stemming from the 2nd and 3rd harmonics, as a starting point for building musical scales. The pair of 3rd and 4th harmonics gives the frequency ratio \(\frac 4 3 \) of a forth (corresponding to \(5\) in \(\ZZ_{ 12 }\)), which complements the fifth to an octave and thus gives no new constraints. However, the next pair of harmonics, yielding the “natural” frequency ratio \(\frac 5 4 \) of a major third (corresponding to \(4\) in \(\ZZ_{ 12 }\)), gives rise to a second starting point of constructing a scale.
    At this moment in our construction, the frequency ratios for \(0 \text{,}\) \(4 \text{,}\) \(5 \text{,}\) and \(7 \) are fixed. The next natural steps are to set the frequency ratio of \(9 = 5+4 \in \ZZ_{ 12 }\) to \(\frac 4 3 \cdot \frac 5 4 = \frac 5 3 \) and that of \(11 = 7 + 4 \in \ZZ_{ 12 }\) to \(\frac 3 2 \cdot \frac 5 4 = \frac{ 15 }{ 8 }\text{.}\) With the same reasoning as for the Pythagorean scale, we assign \(2 = 7 + 7 \in \ZZ_{ 12 }\) the frequency ratio \((\frac 3 2)^2 \cdot \frac 1 2 = \frac 9 8 \text{.}\) A musician will now notice that we have constructed frequencies, such that the three major chords (C-E-G, F-A-C, and G-B-D when we start our scale at C) each have frequency ratios \(4:5:6 \text{,}\) which is precisely the ratio in which the major chord appears within a harmonic spectrum.
    The frequency ratio \(\frac{ 16 }{ 15 }\) of \(1 \) follows by symmetry if we want its product with \(\frac{ 15 }{ 8 }\) (the frequency ratio of \(11 \)) to be \(2 \text{.}\) By the same reasoning we assign \(3 \) the frequency ratio \(\frac 65 \text{,}\) then \(8 \) the frequency ration \(\frac 8 5 \text{,}\) and \(10 \) the frequency ratio \(\frac{ 16 }{ 9 }\text{.}\) What remains is the frequency ratio assigned to \(6 \text{,}\) which has several ways to be broken down in \(\ZZ_{ 12 } \text{.}\) Writing \(6 = 4+2 \text{,}\) we choose the frequency ratio \(\frac 5 4 \cdot \frac 9 8 = \frac{ 45 }{ 32 }\text{.}\) This yields one of the most common just scales given in Table 3.2, due to Johannes Kepler and known as Kepler’s Monochord No. 2, transposed down a fifth [1].
    As promised, the numerators and denominators appearing in this scale are on average quite a bit smaller than the ones in Table 3.1. But Kepler’s just scale has a different set of disadvantages; possibly the most famous is the wolf fifth between D and A, with a frequency ratio of \(\frac 5 3 \cdot \frac 8 9 = \frac{ 40 }{ 27 }\text{,}\) which is a bit off from \(\frac 3 2\text{.}\) Indeed, the ratio of these two values is the famous syntonic comma \(\frac{ 81 }{ 80 }\text{.}\) It shows up all over the place when one studies musical scales; one other instance is the ratio of the frequencies of the Pyathagorean major third (\(\frac{ 81 }{ 64 }\text{,}\) as featured in Table 3.1) and the “natural” major third (\(\frac 5 4 \)) appearing in the harmonic spectrum. The difference of a syntonic comma is noticeable even to non-musically-trained ears.

    Subsection 3.3 Equal-tempered scales and continued fractions

    Our next scale starts out mathematically boring: momentarily throwing out the concept of frequency ratios that are actually fractions, we can easily construct a 12-tone scale in which each semitone interval has exactly the same frequency ratio. Namely, we assign to each interval the appropriate power of \(\sqrt[12] 2\text{.}\) The resulting scale is called equal tempered. A neat mathematical interplay between just and equal-temperament scales is featured in [4].

    Subsection 3.4 Why 12?

    Continued fractions turn out to be useful also when constructing a scale from first principles. Let’s say we want to come up with a scale with \(n\) tones per octave, and we’d like to have a good approximation of the frequency ratio \(\frac 3 2\) for the perfect fifth. If you think about the construction above, this translates to finding a “good” fraction \(\frac a b\) in the sense that \(2^{ \frac a b }\) is reasonably close to \(\frac 3 2\)—in other words, we want to have a good rational approximation of \(\log_2(\frac 3 2)\text{.}\) The continued fraction of this number is as follows:
    This yields as first approximations
    \begin{equation} \frac 1 2, \ \frac 2 3, \ \frac 3 5, \ \frac 4 7, \ \frac 7 {12}, \ \frac{ 24 }{ 41 }, \ \frac{ 31 }{ 53 }, \ \dots \tag{3.1} \end{equation}
    suggesting that there are scales with 2, 3 , 5, 7, 12, 41, 53, etc. tones in an octave that contain a reasonably good perfect fifth.
    Scales with 2 or 3 tones are not that exciting, but any musician will know a pentatonic and a heptatonic scale: as we said earlier, you can hear examples by playing only the black, resp. white,` keys on a piano. These scales are not anywhere close to equally tuned, but those exist also: there is an ancient 5-tone scale tuning system called slendro used in gamelan instruments and a 7-tone scale tuning used in traditional Siamese music [10]. The next numerator in (3.1) is 12, and it is one indication why the 12-tone scale that is prevalent today is mathematically natural.
    You might ask, what about 41- or 53-tone scales? They exist: e.g., there is the xenharmonic garibaldi temperament (using 41 tones) and a generalized keyboard hamonium with 53 notes to an octave constructed in 1876 by Robert Boranquet [10].
